Australia's Baby Drought

Why Australians are having fewer children than ever.

Part 1

Total Fertility Rate

Australia's fertility rate reached a record low in 2024, sitting well below the 2.1 births per woman needed for population replacement.

The ideal replacement level 2.1 births per woman
1.48 children per woman in 2024 Lowest since records began

Total fertility rate, 1927-2024

Children per woman

Australia's total fertility rate has fallen to a record low of 1.48 children per woman in 2024 — less than half the Baby Boom peak of 3.55 reached in 1961. The long decline began almost immediately after the contraceptive pill became widely available in the early 1960s, with the rate dropping below the replacement level of 2.1 for the first time in 1976. A brief recovery followed the introduction of the Federal Government's Baby Bonus in 2004, which provided a one-off payment of up to $5,000 per birth — but the effect proved temporary. The scheme was phased out on 1 March 2014 and replaced by the Newborn Supplement, a more targeted payment tied to the parental leave system. Since then, the fertility rate has fallen sharply and without interruption, reaching its lowest point on record in 2024.

The decline is national, but its pace differs sharply between states and territories.

Every state and territory recorded a lower fertility rate in 2024 than in 2014. The ACT experienced the steepest fall, dropping from 1.738 to 1.273 children per woman, a decline of 26.8%. Western Australia, Tasmania, Queensland and South Australia also fell by more than one-fifth over the decade. The Northern Territory declined the least, from 2.041 to 1.629, and remains the highest-fertility jurisdiction in 2024. The dashed Aust. line marks the national 2024 average of 1.481. By the end of the decade, most states had converged close to that level, showing that fertility rates are not only falling, but becoming more similar across much of the country.

Part 3

Where Babies Are Born

Fertility rates vary sharply at the local level — from inner-city lows to remote community highs — far beyond what state averages reveal.

LGA fertility rates, 2024

This map shows how fertility varies between local government areas. Red areas are below the national average, green areas are higher, and grey areas have too few births for a published rate.

White circles mark each state capital and are sized by total births registered in 2024. Hover any local area for its LGA, state, births, and TFR. Unpublished values appear as N/P.

Australia's fertility landscape tells a clear geographic story. The lowest rates are found in the hearts of the major cities: Melbourne's CBD recorded just 0.54 children per woman in 2024 — the lowest of any LGA in the country — followed closely by Adelaide (0.57), Perth (0.61) and Sydney (0.73). These are places where young professionals are concentrated, housing costs are at their peak, and the pace of life leaves little room for raising children.

At the other extreme, the highest fertility rates are found in small, remote Indigenous communities. Cherbourg in Queensland recorded 3.99 — the highest in Australia — followed by Hope Vale (3.95) and Doomadgee (3.40), all in Queensland. These communities have distinct cultural, social and economic circumstances that shape very different family patterns.

In the vast interior of Australia — the outback stretching across the centre of the continent — fertility rates tend to sit in the moderate-to-high range, typically between 2.0 and 3.0. This band runs through central Queensland, outback New South Wales, South Australia's interior and the Northern Territory. Much of this reflects a combination of younger populations, stronger cultural ties to family, and economic conditions that differ markedly from coastal cities. Notably, many of these central LGAs appear unfilled on the map — not because fertility is zero, but because the population is so small that the ABS does not publish a reliable rate.

Birth volume by state, 2024

The bubble map tells a different story from the LGA fertility map. Large states dominate the number of babies born because they have more people, even when their fertility rates are not especially high.

State and territory bubbles are sized by births registered in 2024. Colour still shows the total fertility rate, making the gap between birth volume and fertility rate easier to compare.

Birth volume is concentrated in Australia's most populous states. New South Wales recorded the most births in 2024, with 89,030 babies, followed by Victoria (81,193) and Queensland (58,838). Western Australia sits well behind the three eastern states at 30,541 births, while South Australia recorded 18,444. The smallest totals are in the smaller jurisdictions: Tasmania (5,509), the ACT (5,218) and the Northern Territory (3,521). This shows that the number of babies born is driven mainly by population size rather than fertility rate. The Northern Territory has the highest TFR, but the fewest births; New South Wales has the largest birth count despite sitting below the national fertility average.

Part 4

Motherhood is moving later

Comparing 1976, 2000 and 2024 shows the age profile shifting right: fertility once peaked in the late twenties, crossed over around 2000, and now peaks among women aged 30-34.

The age profile of motherhood has shifted sharply to the right. In 1976, fertility was concentrated in the twenties, with the highest age-specific fertility rate among women aged 25-29 at 146.2 births per 1,000 women, followed closely by women aged 20-24. By 2000, the peak had moved to 30-34, narrowly overtaking 25-29. In 2024, that later pattern is much clearer: women aged 30-34 now record the highest rate at 106.0, while fertility among women aged 20-24 has fallen to just 30.6. At the same time, births among women aged 35-39 and 40-44 have become more common than they were in the 1970s. The overall trend is not just fewer births, but later births.

Age-specific fertility by state, 2024

Age-specific fertility shows where births concentrate in the life course — and how that pattern differs between states.

Where each state’s fertility peaks

The state heatmap shows that the delayed motherhood pattern is now national. In every state and territory, the highest age-specific fertility rate is among women aged 30-34. New South Wales, South Australia and Victoria have the strongest peaks in this age band, all sitting around 111 births per 1,000 women. The ACT has the lowest fertility among younger women, especially at ages 15-19 and 20-24, but its 35-39 rate remains close to the national pattern, showing how strongly births are concentrated later in life. The Northern Territory stands out in the opposite direction: it still peaks at 30-34, but has much higher fertility among teenagers and women in their early twenties than any other jurisdiction. Overall, the heatmap confirms that Australia's fertility decline is not evenly spread across age groups; births have shifted away from the teens and twenties and are now concentrated in the early thirties.
